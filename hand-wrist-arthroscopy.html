Precision Care for Complex Joint Issues

The hand and wrist are marvels of engineering — a complex network of bones, ligaments, tendons, and nerves that enable dexterity, strength, and fine motor skills. When pain, stiffness, or injury affects these joints, it can disrupt daily life profoundly.

Fortunately, Hand and Wrist Arthroscopy offers a minimally invasive, effective way to diagnose and treat many conditions, promoting faster recovery and better outcomes. What is Hand and Wrist Arthroscopy?

Hand and wrist arthroscopy is a surgical procedure that uses a tiny camera called an arthroscope and specialized instruments inserted through small incisions to view and treat problems inside the wrist and hand joints.

Unlike open surgery, arthroscopy requires only small cuts, resulting in less tissue damage, reduced pain, quicker recovery, and minimal scarring. The arthroscope projects magnified images of the joint on a screen, allowing the surgeon to see detailed joint structures and perform precision repairs.

Conditions Treated with Hand and Wrist Arthroscopy
  • Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (in selected cases)
  • Ganglion cyst removal
  • TFCC (Triangular Fibrocartilage Complex) tears and repairs
  • Wrist fractures and ligament injuries
  • Arthritis and joint degeneration
  • Wrist synovitis (inflammation of the joint lining)
  • Loose bodies inside the wrist joint
  • Scaphoid fractures and nonunions
  • Kienbock’s disease (avascular necrosis of the lunate bone)
  • Diagnostic arthroscopy when imaging is inconclusive

How is the Procedure Performed?
Preoperative Preparation

Before surgery, your doctor will:

  • Review your medical history and symptoms
  • Perform a detailed physical examination
  • Order imaging studies like X-rays, MRI, or CT scans
  • Discuss anesthesia options and procedure risks
Surgical Steps
  • Surgery is typically performed under local or regional anesthesia with sedation, or general anesthesia based on the complexity.
  • Small incisions (usually 2–3) are made on the wrist or hand to insert the arthroscope and instruments.
  • The surgeon inspects joint surfaces, cartilage, ligaments, and bones through the arthroscope.
  • Treatment may include removing inflamed tissue, repairing torn ligaments, excising cysts, smoothing damaged cartilage, or extracting loose fragments.
  • Procedure duration generally ranges from 30 to 90 minutes.
Recovery and Rehabilitation
Immediately After Surgery
  • Mild swelling and discomfort are normal and managed with pain medications and ice packs.
  • Your wrist or hand may be immobilized with a splint or bandage to protect the surgical area.
  • Elevation of the limb is recommended to reduce swelling.
First Few Weeks
  • Follow-up visit for wound inspection and suture removal after 10–14 days.
  • Gradual increase in wrist and finger movement as tolerated.
  • Gentle range-of-motion exercises guided by a physiotherapist or occupational therapist.
Weeks 4 to 12
  • Progressive strengthening and functional exercises to restore grip strength and dexterity.
  • Most patients can resume light daily activities within 4-6 weeks.
  • Return to sports or heavy work may take up to 3 months depending on injury severity.
Benefits of Hand and Wrist Arthroscopy
  • Minimal scarring and tissue trauma
  • Faster recovery compared to open surgery
  • Lower risk of infection and complications
  • Ability to diagnose and treat simultaneously
  • Shorter hospital stay, often outpatient
  • Precise visualization of intricate joint structures
What to Expect After Treatment?

Postoperative results vary based on the initial condition treated and your body’s healing response, but most patients experience:

  • Reduction or elimination of pain and inflammation
  • Improved wrist or hand function and mobility
  • Gradual return of strength and fine motor skills
  • Ability to resume work and daily activities without discomfort
  • Personalized rehabilitation ensures long-term joint health
Who Should Consider Hand and Wrist Arthroscopy?
You may benefit from this procedure if you have:
  • Chronic wrist or hand pain unresponsive to medication or physiotherapy
  • Mechanical symptoms like catching, locking, or instability
  • Confirmed or suspected ligament tears, cartilage damage, or cysts
  • Persistent swelling or stiffness without clear diagnosis
  • Previous wrist injuries requiring detailed assessment and repair
