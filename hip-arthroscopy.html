Advanced Minimally Invasive Treatment for Hip Joint Disorders

The hip joint plays a crucial role in supporting your body’s weight and enabling movement such as walking, running, and jumping. When hip pain or dysfunction arises, it can significantly limit your mobility and quality of life. Fortunately, hip arthroscopy offers a state-of-the-art, minimally invasive surgical solution to diagnose and treat various hip conditions effectively.

For patients seeking the best hip arthroscopy in Bangalore, expert orthopedic surgeons like Dr. Chetan Patil S provide advanced care using the latest techniques and personalized treatment plans.

What is Hip Arthroscopy?

Hip arthroscopy is a minimally invasive surgical procedure that allows doctors to view, diagnose, and treat problems inside the hip joint using a small camera called an arthroscope and tiny surgical instruments. The procedure involves making small incisions (portals) around the hip area through which the arthroscope and instruments are inserted.

The arthroscope transmits real-time images of the inside of the joint onto a monitor, guiding the surgeon to precisely identify and repair issues such as cartilage damage, labral tears, or impingements without the need for large incisions.

Common Conditions Treated with Hip Arthroscopy

Hip arthroscopy is suitable for treating a variety of hip disorders, including:

  • Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI): Abnormal contact between the hip ball and socket causing pain and restricted motion
  • Labral Tears: Damage to the cartilage rim surrounding the hip socket, often causing pain and instability
  • Hip Dysplasia: Shallow hip socket causing joint instability and pain
  • Loose Bodies: Small fragments of bone or cartilage floating inside the joint causing irritation
  • Synovitis: Inflammation of the joint lining
  • Cartilage Damage and Early Osteoarthritis
  • Snapping Hip Syndrome: Tendon or muscle catching on bony structures causing snapping sensations
  • Hip Joint Infections (diagnostic and therapeutic purposes)

How is Hip Arthroscopy Performed?
Preoperative Preparation
  • Comprehensive evaluation and imaging to understand the exact problem
  • Discuss anesthesia options, usually general anesthesia is preferred
  • Pre-surgical instructions including fasting and medication management
Surgical Procedure
  • The patient is positioned on a specialized traction table to open the hip joint space
  • Small incisions (around 2-3) are made around the hip area
  • The arthroscope is inserted, and saline is used to expand the joint for better visualization
  • Specialized instruments are introduced to repair cartilage, trim bone, remove loose bodies, or repair labral tears
  • The procedure typically lasts 1 to 2 hours depending on complexity
  • Incisions are closed with sutures or surgical tape
Recovery and Rehabilitation
Immediate Postoperative Phase
  • Mild to moderate pain and swelling are common, managed with medications and ice packs
  • Hip is often immobilized or protected with crutches to avoid weight-bearing for a few days
  • Hospital stay is usually short, often same-day discharge or overnight stay
First Few Weeks
  • Gradual increase in weight-bearing as advised by the surgeon
  • Physical therapy begins to restore hip motion and prevent stiffness
  • Gentle range of motion and strengthening exercises
Long-term Rehabilitation
  • Progressive strengthening and conditioning exercises to improve hip stability and function
  • Return to normal activities usually within 6 to 12 weeks depending on the procedure and patient compliance
  • Sports and heavy physical work can resume after 3 to 6 months
Benefits of Hip Arthroscopy
  • Minimally invasive with smaller incisions and less tissue trauma
  • Reduced postoperative pain and faster recovery compared to open surgery
  • Accurate diagnosis and simultaneous treatment of intra-articular hip problems
  • Less risk of infection and complications
  • Preservation of healthy joint structures for long-term joint health
What to Expect After Treatment?

Most patients experience significant pain relief, improved hip function, and enhanced quality of life after hip arthroscopy. However, outcomes depend on factors such as:

  • Severity and type of hip pathology
  • Adherence to rehabilitation and physiotherapy protocols
  • Overall health and activity level

Who Should Consider Hip Arthroscopy?

Consider hip arthroscopy if you have:

  • Persistent hip pain limiting daily activities
  • Mechanical symptoms such as clicking, locking, or instability
  • Diagnosed labral tears, FAI, or cartilage injuries not responding to conservative treatments
  • Early degenerative changes in the hip joint
  • Need for diagnostic evaluation when imaging is inconclusive
