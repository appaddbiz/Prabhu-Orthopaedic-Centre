Minimally Invasive Surgery for Knee Joint Disorders

The knee is one of the most important and complex joints in the human body, enabling activities such as walking, running, and jumping. When knee pain, swelling, or mobility issues arise due to injury or degenerative conditions, knee arthroscopy offers an advanced minimally invasive surgical option to diagnose and treat various knee problems effectively.

For those seeking the best knee arthroscopy in Bangalore, expert orthopedic surgeons like Dr. Chetan Patil S provide state-of-the-art care using the latest arthroscopic techniques and personalized treatment plans.

What is Knee Arthroscopy?

Knee arthroscopy is a surgical procedure that uses a small camera called an arthroscope to look inside the knee joint. Through tiny incisions, the surgeon inserts the arthroscope and specialized surgical instruments to examine, diagnose, and treat problems within the knee.

This minimally invasive approach allows the surgeon to repair damaged tissues such as torn menisci, cartilage injuries, or ligament tears, and remove loose fragments inside the joint, all without the need for large incisions.

Common Conditions Treated with Knee Arthroscopy
  • Meniscal Tears: Damage to the cartilage cushions between the thigh and shin bones
  • Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL) Injuries: Partial or complete ligament tears causing instability
  • Cartilage Damage or Defects: Wear and tear or injury to the cartilage surfaces
  • Loose Bodies: Small fragments of bone or cartilage floating inside the joint causing pain or locking
  • Patellar (Kneecap) Disorders: Misalignment or cartilage damage beneath the kneecap
  • Synovitis: Inflammation of the joint lining causing swelling and pain
  • Removal of inflamed or damaged tissue
  • Baker’s Cyst Drainage in some cases

How is Knee Arthroscopy Performed?
Preoperative Preparation
  • Detailed evaluation and imaging to determine the exact pathology
  • Instructions regarding fasting and medication management before surgery
  • General or regional anesthesia is usually administered
Surgical Procedure
  • The patient is positioned lying down with the knee flexed slightly
  • Small incisions (portals) approximately 0.5-1 cm are made around the knee
  • The arthroscope is inserted, and sterile fluid is used to expand the joint for clear visibility
  • Surgical instruments are inserted through additional portals to repair or remove damaged tissues
  • The procedure usually lasts 30 minutes to 2 hours depending on the complexity
  • Incisions are closed with sutures or adhesive strips
Recovery and Rehabilitation
Immediately After Surgery
  • Mild to moderate pain, swelling, and stiffness are common and managed with medications and ice therapy
  • Patients may use crutches for a few days to reduce weight-bearing on the knee
  • Most patients can go home the same day or the next day after surgery
First Few Weeks
  • Early mobilization and gentle range of motion exercises begin under physiotherapist guidance
  • Gradual increase in weight-bearing as tolerated
  • Swelling and pain continue to decrease with proper care
Long-term Rehabilitation
  • Progressive strengthening of muscles around the knee to restore stability
  • Balance and proprioception exercises to improve joint function
  • Return to normal daily activities typically within 4 to 6 weeks
  • Sports and physically demanding activities may resume gradually under professional supervision
Benefits of Knee Arthroscopy
  • Minimally invasive with smaller incisions and minimal tissue trauma
  • Faster recovery and less postoperative pain compared to open knee surgery
  • Accurate diagnosis and simultaneous treatment of knee joint problems
  • Lower risk of infection and complications
  • Short hospital stay and quicker return to daily activities
What to Expect After Treatment?

Most patients report significant improvement in knee pain, mobility, and function after knee arthroscopy. Success depends on:

  • Severity and type of knee injury or condition
  • Adherence to rehabilitation protocols and physical therapy
  • Overall health and lifestyle choices

Who is a Candidate for Knee Arthroscopy?
  • Persistent knee pain not responding to conservative treatments like medication or physiotherapy
  • Mechanical symptoms such as locking, catching, or instability
  • Diagnosed meniscal tears, cartilage damage, or ligament injuries
  • Early-stage arthritis causing joint discomfort
  • Need for diagnostic evaluation when imaging results are inconclusive
