Advanced Minimally Invasive Treatment for Joint Health

Pain, stiffness, or instability in the foot and ankle can severely affect your ability to walk, stand, and perform daily activities. In cases where conservative treatments fail, Foot and Ankle Arthroscopy can be a highly effective, minimally invasive procedure to diagnose and treat a wide range of joint problems.

What is Foot and Ankle Arthroscopy?

Foot and ankle arthroscopy is a minimally invasive surgical procedure that allows an orthopedic surgeon to examine, diagnose, and treat conditions affecting the foot and ankle joints. It involves making small incisions through which a tiny camera (arthroscope) and specialized instruments are inserted.

The arthroscope displays real-time images on a screen, enabling the surgeon to assess joint surfaces, cartilage damage, and soft tissue injuries. Treatments can also be performed during the same procedure, reducing the need for larger incisions and promoting quicker recovery.

Conditions Treated with Foot and Ankle Arthroscopy

Arthroscopy is suitable for both diagnostic and therapeutic purposes. Common conditions treated through this procedure include:

  • Ankle impingement (bone spurs causing pinching)
  • Cartilage injuries or osteochondral defects
  • Loose bodies (bone or cartilage fragments)
  • Synovitis (inflammation of the joint lining)
  • Ligament injuries and ankle instability
  • Arthritis or degenerative joint disease
  • Posterior impingement syndrome (common in ballet dancers and athletes)
  • Anterior ankle pain and stiffness
  • Infection requiring joint washout
  • Scar tissue removal after trauma or previous surgeries

If you're experiencing persistent foot or ankle pain, swelling, or restricted motion, arthroscopy could offer the solution.

The Arthroscopic Procedure: What to Expect
1. Pre-Operative Evaluation
Before surgery, a complete assessment is conducted, including:
  • Medical history and physical examination
  • Imaging studies such as X-rays, MRI, or CT scan
  • Functional evaluation of joint movement and pain
2. During the Procedure
  • Performed under general or regional anesthesia
  • Small incisions (portals) made around the ankle or foot
  • Arthroscope inserted to inspect the joint
  • Surgical tools used to remove loose fragments, smooth cartilage, or repair damaged tissue
  • Procedure duration: 30 to 90 minutes
3. Post-Surgery
  • Patients are often discharged the same day
  • Bandages and splints may be applied for protection
  • Pain management and antibiotics as needed
Recovery and Rehabilitation After Foot and Ankle Arthroscopy

Recovery timelines vary based on the extent of the condition treated and patient response. However, arthroscopy generally enables quicker healing compared to open surgery.

First Week Post-Surgery
  • Use of crutches or walker may be necessary
  • Elevation of the foot and cold therapy to reduce swelling
  • Mild pain or stiffness is common
  • Gentle movement exercises guided by your doctor
Weeks 2–6
  • Sutures removed after 10–14 days
  • Gradual return to weight-bearing as advised
  • Start of structured physiotherapy
  • Range-of-motion exercises and light strengthening
  • Continued monitoring by Dr. Chetan Patil S
Weeks 6–12
  • Improved mobility and reduced pain
  • Restoration of foot/ankle function
  • Return to normal footwear
  • Possible return to sports or heavy activities depending on healing

When is Foot and Ankle Arthroscopy Recommended?

You may be advised to undergo arthroscopy if:

  • Conservative treatments (rest, physiotherapy, injections) have failed
  • You suffer from chronic ankle pain or swelling
  • You experience mechanical symptoms like catching, locking, or instability
  • There is known cartilage damage or joint degeneration
  • You need a clear diagnosis for unexplained joint pain

Early diagnosis and arthroscopic intervention can prevent further joint damage and help you return to a pain-free lifestyle.
