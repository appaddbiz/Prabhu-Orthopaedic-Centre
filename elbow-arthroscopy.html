What is Elbow Arthroscopy?

Elbow arthroscopy is a minimally invasive surgical procedure used to diagnose and treat various problems inside the elbow joint. Using a small camera called an arthroscope, the orthopedic surgeon examines the joint internally through tiny incisions and treats the issue with micro-instruments.

This advanced technique allows for quicker recovery, less pain, and fewer complications than traditional open surgery.

Conditions Treated with Elbow Arthroscopy

Elbow arthroscopy is recommended for both diagnostic and therapeutic purposes. It is commonly used to address:

  • Tennis elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis) – when non-surgical treatments fail
  • Elbow arthritis – removal of bone spurs, loose cartilage, or inflamed tissue
  • Osteochondritis dissecans (OCD) – common in athletes
  • Loose bodies – cartilage or bone fragments causing joint locking
  • Synovitis – inflammation of the elbow lining
  • Joint stiffness or contracture
  • Instability or ligament damage
  • Post-traumatic injuries
  • Fracture cleanup after elbow trauma

Patients experiencing persistent elbow pain, swelling, locking, or restricted motion can greatly benefit from elbow arthroscopy.

The Elbow Arthroscopy Procedure: What to Expect
1. Pre-Surgical Evaluation
Your consultation begins with:
  • Detailed history and physical exam
  • Imaging studies such as X-rays, MRI, or CT scans
  • Assessment of joint movement and pain triggers
2. Surgical Preparation
  • Procedure is typically done under general or regional anesthesia
  • Outpatient surgery – patients can often go home the same day
  • Small incisions (usually 2–4) made around the elbow
  • Arthroscope and instruments inserted for visualization and treatment
3. Duration and Procedure Steps
  • Surgery duration: 45 minutes to 1.5 hours
  • Removal of bone spurs or loose fragments
  • Tendon debridement or ligament repair
  • Synovectomy (removal of inflamed tissue)
  • Capsular release (to restore motion)
Recovery and Rehabilitation After Elbow Arthroscopy

Recovery is usually faster than open elbow surgery. However, it depends on the underlying condition treated and patient compliance with post-operative care.

First Week After Surgery
  • Elbow may be bandaged and supported in a sling
  • Ice packs and pain medication help manage swelling and discomfort
  • Elevation of the arm reduces swelling
  • Start gentle range-of-motion exercises as directed
Weeks 2–6
  • Sutures removed within 10–14 days
  • Guided physiotherapy to restore motion and strength
  • Focus on reducing stiffness and regaining flexibility
  • Return to light work or daily activities
6 Weeks and Beyond
  • Gradual return to sports, lifting, or physically demanding tasks
  • Full recovery may take 3–4 months, depending on the complexity of surgery
  • Continued physiotherapy ensures long-term joint health and function

When Should You Consider Elbow Arthroscopy?

You may be a candidate for elbow arthroscopy if you:

  • Have chronic elbow pain or swelling that limits activity
  • Suffer from joint locking or stiffness
  • Have failed to respond to conservative treatments like medication or physiotherapy
  • Have mechanical symptoms like catching, clicking, or instability
  • Are recovering from an elbow injury that has led to complications

Early diagnosis and timely surgical intervention can prevent long-term damage and improve quality of life.
