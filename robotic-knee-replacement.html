What Is Robotic Knee Replacement?

Robotic Knee Replacement is an advanced form of knee replacement surgery in which a robotic-assisted system helps the surgeon perform the procedure with enhanced precision. Contrary to common misconceptions, the robot does not operate independently. Instead, it is a highly sophisticated tool that assists your surgeon in executing a pre-planned, patient-specific surgical approach.

How It Works:

  • Pre-Surgical Planning: A CT scan or 3D model of your knee is used to create a personalized surgical plan.
  • Precision Execution: During surgery, the robotic system assists in making highly accurate bone cuts and optimal placement of implants.
  • Real-Time Feedback: The system gives real-time data to adjust and align the implant perfectly.

This advanced approach significantly reduces the margin of human error and enhances the longevity and functionality of the new knee joint.

Why Choose Robotic Knee Replacement?
  • Superior Precision: Ensures ideal alignment and implant placement.
  • Customized Surgery: Tailored to your knee’s unique anatomy.
  • Minimally Invasive: Smaller incisions mean reduced blood loss and less post-op pain.
  • Faster Recovery: Patients walk earlier and resume normal activities sooner.
  • Longer Implant Lifespan: Reduced wear and tear on the joint over time.
  • Fewer Complications: Lower risk of implant failure or revision surgery.

These benefits make robotic surgery a game-changer in modern joint replacement care.

Conditions That Require Robotic Knee Replacement
  • Osteoarthritis of the knee
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Post-traumatic arthritis
  • Knee deformities
  • Failed previous knee surgeries
  • Severe joint degeneration with chronic pain

If these conditions limit your mobility and quality of life, and conservative treatments no longer help, robotic knee replacement may be recommended.

The Robotic Surgery Process
Before Surgery:
  • Pre-operative tests
  • CT scan to map your knee structure
  • Medical fitness clearance
  • Patient education about the procedure and recovery
During Surgery:
  • General or spinal anesthesia
  • Robotic system maps out the bone structure in real-time
  • Precise removal of damaged bone and cartilage
  • Accurate positioning of the implant using robotic guidance
  • Surgery duration: 1.5–2 hours
After Surgery:
  • Early mobilization within 24 hours
  • Pain management and physical therapy initiation
Recovery and Rehabilitation
Week 1–2:
  • Walking with walker or crutches
  • Pain and swelling management
  • Physiotherapy begins in-hospital or at home
Week 3–6:
  • Walking with minimal support
  • Improved knee range of motion
  • Climbing stairs and light daily activities
After 6 Weeks:
  • Driving and light exercises resume
  • Return to desk jobs and low-impact activities
3–6 Months:
  • Most patients resume full, active lifestyles
  • Continued strength training for muscle support
